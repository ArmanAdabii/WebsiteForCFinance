Petrobras.

Energy for a society in motion.

Company overview:

In 1953 Petrobras was established by the "Oil is ours" campaign, President Getulio Vargas signed Law No. 2,004, which created Petrobras. Fully nationally controlled and owned, with a majority interest of the Federal Government, Petrobras has a monopoly on the exploration of all stages of the oil industry, with the exception of distribution.

In 1997, Law 9.478 ended the monopoly, allowing Petrobras to enter joint ventures with international oil companies (IOCs). This forced a shift toward commercial efficiency.

In 2006, Petrobras discovered the Tupi (Lula) field which revolutionized the company. Petrobras became a world leader in ultra deep water technology accessing 7000 meters below sea levels. Also, Petrobras reached sustainable self-sufficiency in Brazil for oil and gas production with the operation of the P-34 and P-50 platforms, which projects were developed by Cenpes. With average daily production of 1.9 million barrels per day, the country started exporting more oil and byproducts than importing them.

2015- 2016 Crises and recovery: Following a period of extreme debt (peaking $126B) and political challenges, the company utilized a “Value over volume” strategy which involved the sell of non-core assets (divestment) and focusing strictly on high margin deep water production. Mature onshore fields, shallow-water platforms, and some refineries. These were "volume" assets but had low margins.

Integrated business model:

Petrobras operates across the entire value chain, but the current operations are primarily focused:

  • Upstream (The core): 90%+ of the capital expenditure is focused on exploration and production. The primary engine is the pre-salt fields which offers low lifting costs, and high class productivity.
  • Downstream (The market): Petrobras is the most dominant energy and refining company in Brazil that controls 10+ oil refineries. This allows them to capture margin from the oil well to the gas station.
  • Sustainability transition: There is a growing focus on decarbonizing operations aiming for net zero emissions for the year 2050 while for now the main cash driver is oil.

Competitive landscape:

In the global market Petrobras competes with ExxonMobil, Chevron, TotalEnergies, and Shell.

Competitive Advantage:

Petrobras has low lifting costs while other peers are struggling with maturing fields, Petrobras’s pre-salt field offers a “break even” resilience, which means total revenue (money earned) equals total costs (money spent). This allows Petrobras to profit even when the price of oil drops to $35 dollars per barrel.

National role:

Petrobras must maintain a dual mandate: deliver dividends to shareholders and fulfill Brazilian government’s strategic energy goals.

Segment Performance (2023)

A breakdown of the business segments reveals that Exploration & Production (Upstream) is the primary profit engine. While the Downstream segment generates high gross revenue, the Upstream segment accounts for the vast majority of operating income due to the elite efficiency of Pre-salt extraction.
