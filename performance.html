Financial Analysis

Petrobras has demonstrated excellent performance and operational efficiency, as seen by the significant operating margin derived from its pre-salt salt assets. The company's operational margin as of 2024 is an amazing 28.1%, compared to the industry median of 4.65%. The gross profit margin of 50.29%, which indicates that the business keeps half of its income after production costs, better captures this efficiency. Even after taxes and interest, Petrobras's net profit margin of 8.32% surpasses the industry average of 1.75%.

The company's liquidity indicates that, in contrast to its strong profitability, it has a very active and lean cash management plan. With a current ratio of 0.69, the company's short-term commitments are currently greater than its short-term assets. For a major corporation like Petrobras, such indications indicate that the company prioritizes large dividend payments and debt obligations, but they also frequently indicate difficulties in other industries. This is supported by a 33.41 days of cash conversion cycle which is very close to the industry median.

Deleveraging

The most compelling part of this analysis is how Petrobras managed the debt crisis it had. In 2015 Petrobras started a massive deleveraging program successfully reducing debt to equity ratio from 1.71 to 0.83 in 2024, and this stability is underscored by an interest coverage ratio of 3.9x which shows that it generates nearly four times the profit required to service its debt. Inventory Management

Inventory turnover, this metric shows how many times a year Petrobras sells through its whole inventory and stock of oil. The median is 17.7 but PBR is 6.4 which shows that the company holds oil longer than the industry median.

Cashflow Analysis

Cash from operating activities: This figure shows the actual money from pumping and selling oil. Petrobras had a stable improvement averaging $26B every year until 2022. Even in 2022 during the global energy crisis, Petrobras managed to generate $49.7B, and take advantage of the crisis.

Investing activities and CapEx: Capital expenditure was managed very well, especially in 2020- 2021 ($6B) before rising to $14.6B in 2024. Petrobras’s strategy is only to spend on high return and deep water projects rather than inefficient infrastructure. The 2021 Divestment program usually shows investment as negative (spending money), but in 2021 it shows as $2.2B, and this positive inflow is explained by divestment where Petrobras sold non-core assets.

Financing activities: The data shows the company had massive outflow and peaking -$51.5B in 2022 which represents the company’s commitment to paying down debt and rewarding shareholders with dividends which transformed the company from a highly indebted firm to a cash generating one.

Free cash flow: Petrobras' FCF was only $0.2 billion in 2015, but it changed to $44.8 billion in 2022 and, even after normalization, to $25.1 billion in 2024. The DFCF estimated price is higher because of the large amount of remaining funds. The company is generating more surplus cash than almost any emerging market peers.
